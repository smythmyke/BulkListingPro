I relisted Etsy products by hand for 6 months. Here's what I'd do differently.

Half a year of Etsy's new-listing form. What I wasted time on, what actually mattered, and the one decision I should have made sooner.

Michael Smyth
April 18, 2026
TL;DR

Six months of manual Etsy listing taught me three things I'd change: stop writing titles and tags by hand (AI does it better than tired me), batch the work by task type instead of by product, and stop treating listing day as creative work. The first two are tooling; the third is mindset.

I used to think of Etsy listing as the "business" half of being a seller. The making was the fun part; the listing was the tax I paid to have someone buy what I made. For six months I paid that tax by hand, one new-listing form at a time, and only realized at the end how much of it didn't have to be that way.

This isn't a tools-review piece. It's the retrospective I wish I'd read six months earlier — a breakdown of where the time actually went, what was wasted, and the specific moves I'd make differently.

What I actually did for six months

My routine: open Etsy's new-listing form, paste or type a title, upload the image, upload the digital file, pick a category (guess, usually), type thirteen tags from memory, write a description from a mental template, set the price, save, close, open the next one.

Five to eight minutes per listing if I was focused. Ten to fifteen if I was drifting. I listed between five and twenty products a week depending on what I'd made that week. Over six months that's somewhere around 300-400 listings, most of which involved me doing the same mechanical motions I'd done for the previous 299.

Where the time actually went

If I had to reconstruct where those minutes went, the breakdown was roughly:

  • Writing titles — 25% of time, highest decision fatigue
  • Picking tags — 20% of time, low confidence in outputs
  • Writing descriptions — 15% of time, same template every time
  • Image upload and crop — 15% of time, pure clicking
  • Category picking and form navigation — 15% of time
  • Everything else — 10% of time

Notice the pattern: the top three items (60% of my time) were all writing tasks. Not product work, not creative work — just writing slightly different variations of similar text over and over.

What I'd do differently, in order of impact

1. Stop writing titles and tags manually

This is the big one. After six months I can say honestly: my manually-written titles were no better than AI-drafted ones would have been. I just had to tire myself out writing them. Half were clean SEO efforts; the other half were lazy riffs on previous titles because I'd run out of creative energy.

An AI title that I edit with ten seconds of attention is typically better than a manual title I wrote on hour three of a listing session. AI doesn't get tired. I do.

2. Batch by task, not by product

I was doing the equivalent of cooking dinner by washing one potato, peeling it, chopping it, boiling it, then starting the next potato. Silly when you say it out loud about food. Equally silly when you say it about listings.

The fix was obvious in retrospect: do all the CSV prep at once, then all the AI writing at once (or manual writing if you prefer), then all the review at once, then kick off the upload and walk away. The output is the same. The evening is half as draining.

3. Stop treating listing as creative work

I used to try to "come up with" good titles during listing sessions. Bad idea. Creative work and execution work need different brain states. I was trying to do creative work while tired from executing, which meant the creative work was bad and the execution was slow.

Now I split them. Idea work happens in a separate block, usually earlier in the day. Listing is pure execution, and it's faster because I've stopped pretending it's anything else.

The mistake wasn't that listing was hard. It was treating listing like it required my full creative attention when most of it just needed my full mechanical attention.

The one thing I'd leave the same

Review. Even with AI generation handling most of the writing, someone still needs to look at the output and make judgment calls on which titles need a human edit. That's not something to automate away. It's the 10% of listing work that actually benefits from human attention.

Everything else is mechanical. Review is judgment. Keep the judgment, lose the mechanics.

The real cost of six months of manual listing

I don't mostly regret the time spent. I regret what I wasn't doing during that time. For every hour I spent typing titles into Etsy's form, I wasn't making new designs, not improving my existing products, not engaging with buyers, not thinking about where my shop should go next.

The opportunity cost is the hidden fee on manual work that nobody quotes. The listing fee is 20 cents. The time fee — in creative output foregone — is substantially more.

What I'd tell past me

Something like: "You're not going to regret automating the listing part. You're going to regret how long you waited." I probably wouldn't have listened. It took six months of doing it the hard way to appreciate how hard the hard way actually was.

Maybe the only useful thing about doing something manually for too long is the clarity it gives you about what was actually valuable in the work — and what was just you paying a tax you could have paid once, not every day.

Frequently asked questions

How long does it really take to list one Etsy product manually?

Five to eight minutes per listing if nothing goes wrong. Ten to fifteen minutes if you're writing a thoughtful title, picking tags carefully, and cropping images.

What's the worst part of manual Etsy listing?

Not the individual steps — each is easy. The mode-switching between them. Your brain pays a tax each time.

Can you realistically list 100 products a week manually?

Yes, but at a serious quality cost. Output quality degrades exponentially with fatigue.

What's the one thing you'd change about your old workflow?

Stop writing titles and tags manually. That was 60% of my time and the output wasn't better than AI-drafted content after a quick review pass.
